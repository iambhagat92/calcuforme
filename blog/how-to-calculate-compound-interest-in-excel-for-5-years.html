Excel Methods to Calculate Compound Interest for 5 Years

Method 1: Using FV Function (Recommended)

The FV (Future Value) function is the easiest way to calculate compound interest in Excel for 5 years:

=FV(rate/compounding_frequency, compounding_frequency*5, 0, -principal)

Example: â‚¹5,00,000 at 8% quarterly for 5 years:

=FV(8%/4, 4*5, 0, -500000)

Result: â‚¹7,42,974.70

Method 2: POWER Function Formula

Direct implementation of the compound interest formula:

=Principal * POWER((1 + Rate/Frequency), Frequency*5)

Example with cell references:

=A1 * POWER((1 + B1/C1), C1*5)

Where A1=Principal, B1=Annual Rate, C1=Compounding Frequency

FAQs: How to Calculate Compound Interest in Excel for 5 Years

Q1: Which Excel function is best for 5-year compound interest?

The FV function is most efficient: =FV(rate/n, n*5, 0, -principal). It's built specifically for financial calculations and handles all compounding scenarios accurately.

Q2: How much difference does monthly vs yearly compounding make over 5 years?

On average, monthly compounding adds 1-2% extra returns over 5 years. For â‚¹10 lakhs at 8%, yearly gives â‚¹14,69,328 while monthly gives â‚¹14,89,845 - a difference of â‚¹20,517.

Q3: Should I choose 5-year FD or other investment options?

5-year FDs offer guaranteed returns and tax benefits under 80C. However, equity mutual funds historically provide 12-15% returns over 5 years but with higher risk. PPF offers tax-free returns but lower rates.

Q4: How to account for inflation in 5-year calculations?

Real return formula: =(1+nominal_rate)/(1+inflation_rate)-1. If your FD gives 7.5% and inflation is 5%, real return is only 2.38% per year.
