How to Calculate Years, Months and Days in Excel Between Two Dates

Master Excel date calculations with DATEDIF and other powerful formulas - Complete step-by-step guide

Published: January 2025 | 12 min read | Excel Tutorial

Why Calculate Date Differences in Excel?

Calculating the difference between two dates in years, months, and days is one of the most common tasks in Excel. Whether you're tracking employee tenure, calculating age, measuring project duration, or managing subscription periods, knowing how to accurately calculate date differences is essential.

üí° Quick Fact: Excel stores dates as sequential numbers starting from January 1, 1900. This makes date calculations possible using simple arithmetic and specialized functions.

Method 1: Using the DATEDIF Function (Most Popular)

The DATEDIF function is the most powerful tool for calculating date differences in Excel. Although it's not documented in Excel's help system, it's been available since Excel 97 and works perfectly.

DATEDIF Syntax:

=DATEDIF(start_date, end_date, unit)

DATEDIF Unit Parameters:

Unit What It Returns Example
"Y" Complete years 2 years
"M" Complete months 25 months
"D" Total days 762 days
"YM" Months ignoring years 1 month (remainder)
"YD" Days ignoring years 45 days (remainder)
"MD" Days ignoring months & years 15 days (remainder)

Complete Formula to Calculate Years, Months, and Days

Example Scenario:

Start Date (Cell A2): January 15, 2020
End Date (Cell B2): October 30, 2025
We want: 5 Years, 9 Months, and 15 Days

Step-by-Step Formula Breakdown:

1. Calculate Complete Years:

=DATEDIF(A2, B2, "Y")

Returns: 5 (complete years)

2. Calculate Remaining Months (after years):

=DATEDIF(A2, B2, "YM")

Returns: 9 (months remaining after complete years)

3. Calculate Remaining Days (after months):

=DATEDIF(A2, B2, "MD")

Returns: 15 (days remaining after complete months)

Combined Formula with Text:

=DATEDIF(A2,B2,"Y") & " Years, " & DATEDIF(A2,B2,"YM") & " Months, " & DATEDIF(A2,B2,"MD") & " Days"

Result: "5 Years, 9 Months, 15 Days"

Method 2: Manual Calculation Using YEAR, MONTH, and DAY Functions

If you prefer not to use DATEDIF (or if you need more control), you can manually calculate the difference:

Calculate Years:

=YEAR(B2) - YEAR(A2)

Calculate Months (more complex):

=IF(DAY(B2)>=DAY(A2), MONTH(B2)-MONTH(A2), MONTH(B2)-MONTH(A2)-1)

Calculate Days (most complex):

=IF(DAY(B2)>=DAY(A2), DAY(B2)-DAY(A2), DAY(EOMONTH(A2,0))-DAY(A2)+DAY(B2))
‚ö†Ô∏è Note: Manual calculation is more error-prone and doesn't handle leap years and varying month lengths as accurately as DATEDIF. We recommend using DATEDIF whenever possible.

Method 3: Calculate Total Duration in Single Units

Total Days Between Two Dates:

=B2 - A2

Or use: =DATEDIF(A2, B2, "D")

Total Complete Months:

=DATEDIF(A2, B2, "M")

Total Weeks:

=(B2 - A2) / 7

Decimal Years (for interest calculations):

=YEARFRAC(A2, B2)

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Calculate Employee Tenure

Scenario: Employee joined on March 15, 2018. Today is October 30, 2025.

=DATEDIF(A2,TODAY(),"Y") & " Years, " & DATEDIF(A2,TODAY(),"YM") & " Months"

Result: "7 Years, 7 Months"

Example 2: Calculate Age from Date of Birth

Scenario: Date of birth: June 10, 1995

=DATEDIF(A2,TODAY(),"Y") & " Years Old"

Result: "30 Years Old"

Example 3: Project Duration

Scenario: Project start: Jan 1, 2024 | Project end: Sep 30, 2025

=DATEDIF(A2,B2,"M") & " Months, " & DATEDIF(A2,B2,"MD") & " Days"

Result: "21 Months, 0 Days"

Common Errors and Troubleshooting

Error: #NUM!

Cause: End date is earlier than start date.
Solution: Make sure your start date comes before your end date. Use: =IF(B2>A2, DATEDIF(A2,B2,"Y"), "Invalid dates")

Error: #NAME?

Cause: DATEDIF function not recognized (usually a typo).
Solution: Double-check spelling. It's DATEDIF, not DATEDIFF.

MD Unit Issues

Known Bug: The "MD" unit in DATEDIF can produce incorrect results in some cases when the start day is greater than the end day.
Workaround: Use: =DAY(B2-DAY(A2)+1) if you encounter issues.

Advanced Tips and Tricks

1. Handle Negative Dates Gracefully:

=IF(B2>A2, DATEDIF(A2,B2,"Y")&" Years, "&DATEDIF(A2,B2,"YM")&" Months", "End date before start")

2. Custom Date Format Display:

=TEXT(DATEDIF(A2,B2,"Y"),"0")&"y "&TEXT(DATEDIF(A2,B2,"YM"),"0")&"m "&TEXT(DATEDIF(A2,B2,"MD"),"0")&"d"

Result: "5y 9m 15d"

3. Exclude Weekends (Business Days Only):

=NETWORKDAYS(A2, B2)

Returns only working days (Monday-Friday)

4. Include Custom Holidays:

=NETWORKDAYS(A2, B2, HolidayRange)

Where HolidayRange contains a list of holiday dates

Frequently Asked Questions

Why doesn't DATEDIF appear in Excel's function list?

DATEDIF is an undocumented function inherited from Lotus 1-2-3 for compatibility. Despite not being in the official documentation, it works reliably in all modern Excel versions.

Can I use DATEDIF in Google Sheets?

Yes! DATEDIF works exactly the same way in Google Sheets with the same syntax and parameters.

How do I calculate age as of a specific date (not today)?

Replace TODAY() with your specific date: =DATEDIF(A2,DATE(2025,12,31),"Y") calculates age as of December 31, 2025.

What if I only want months and days (no years)?

Use: =DATEDIF(A2,B2,"M") & " Months, " & DATEDIF(A2,B2,"MD") & " Days"

Does DATEDIF account for leap years?

Yes, DATEDIF automatically handles leap years, varying month lengths, and all calendar complexities correctly.

Can I calculate backwards (future date to past date)?

No, start_date must be earlier than end_date. If not, you'll get a #NUM! error. Use IF to handle this: =IF(B2>A2, DATEDIF(A2,B2,"Y"), DATEDIF(B2,A2,"Y"))
