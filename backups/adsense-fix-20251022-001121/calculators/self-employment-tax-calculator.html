Understanding Self-Employment Tax

Self-employment tax is a federal tax that consists of Social Security and Medicare taxes for individuals who work for themselves. Unlike traditional employees who split these taxes with their employer, self-employed individuals must pay the full amount themselves.

Current Self-Employment Tax Rates (2025)

  • Social Security Tax: 12.4% on income up to $168,600
  • Medicare Tax: 2.9% on all income
  • Additional Medicare Tax: 0.9% on income over $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)
  • Total Rate: 15.3% for most self-employed individuals

Who Must Pay Self-Employment Tax?

You must pay self-employment tax if you had net earnings from self-employment of $400 or more. This includes:

  • Freelancers and independent contractors
  • Business owners (sole proprietors, partners in partnerships)
  • Gig economy workers (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash drivers)
  • Consultants and professional service providers
  • Online sellers and e-commerce entrepreneurs

How to Calculate Self-Employment Tax

The calculation involves several steps:

  1. Calculate Net Self-Employment Income: Total income minus business expenses
  2. Apply the 92.35% Factor: Multiply by 0.9235 to account for the employer-equivalent portion
  3. Calculate Social Security Tax: Apply 12.4% to income up to the wage base limit
  4. Calculate Medicare Tax: Apply 2.9% to all adjusted income
  5. Add Additional Medicare Tax: 0.9% on income exceeding thresholds

Self-Employment Tax Deductions

The good news is that you can deduct the employer-equivalent portion (50%) of your self-employment tax when calculating your adjusted gross income. This effectively reduces your overall tax burden.

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Since no employer withholds taxes from your self-employment income, you're responsible for making quarterly estimated tax payments to avoid penalties. These payments should include both income tax and self-employment tax.

Important Reminder

Self-employment tax is separate from income tax. Even if you don't owe income tax, you may still owe self-employment tax if your net self-employment earnings are $400 or more.
